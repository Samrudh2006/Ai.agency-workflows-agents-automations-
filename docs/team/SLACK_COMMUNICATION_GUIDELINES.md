# 💬 Slack Communication Guidelines
**How our remote agency stays aligned without constant Zoom calls.**

</div>

<br />

To build AI systems effectively as a distributed team, we must protect "Deep Work" time. This means asynchronous communication is our default.

---

## 🎯 1. Public Default
Except for HR or highly sensitive management issues, **keep all conversations in public channels.**
- Do not DMs a developer asking "How does the Make.com webhook work?"
- Ask it in `#dev-automations`. Someone else might need to know the answer later, and keeping it public creates a searchable knowledge base.

## 🧵 2. Thread It
Never reply to a message in the main channel flow. Always use threads.
- **Why?** Unthreaded replies flood the channel and bury important announcements. Keeping conversations in threads allows people to easily find and follow context.

## 🚨 3. The Urgency Protocol

How to handle issues based on severity:
- **Low Priority (Within 24hrs):** Post in the standard project channel. No tagging required.
- **Medium Priority (Within 4hrs):** Post in the channel and `@mention` the specific person.
- **High Priority (Within 30mins):** Post in `#critical-alerts` and tag `@here` or the specific Lead Dev. (e.g. "The OpenAI API is bouncing 500 errors for Client X!").
- **ABSOLUTE EMERGENCY:** Call their literal phone number. (e.g. "We accidentally pushed Client Y's API key to a public GitHub repo!").

## ❌ 4. Ban on "Hello" and "Hey"
Never send a message that just says "Hey Mark" and wait for them to respond before typing your question. This wastes time.

**Do this instead:**
> "Hey Mark, doing a QA check on the Voice Agent. The latency seems to have spiked to 3 seconds. Do you know if Vapi is having issues right now?"

## 📍 5. Status Icons
Update your Slack status to protect your time:
- 🎧 **Deep Work:** Do not disturb for 2 hours.
- 📞 **On a Client Call:** Available via emergency text only.
- 🌴 **OOO:** Out of Office.
